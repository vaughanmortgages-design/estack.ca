Second Mortgages for Investment Properties in Ontario

Updated June 2026  ·  7 min read  ·  estack.ca editorial team

If you own an investment property in Ontario with meaningful equity, a second mortgage is often the fastest way to access that equity without touching your existing first mortgage — especially if that first mortgage has a great rate you don't want to lose by refinancing.

What a Second Mortgage Actually Is

A second mortgage is a separate loan registered against your property, behind your existing first mortgage. If the property is ever sold or foreclosed, the first mortgage gets paid out before the second. That extra risk to the lender is why second mortgages carry higher rates than a first — but for accessing equity quickly, they're often faster and simpler than a full refinance.

Why investors use seconds specifically: Many investment property owners locked in low rates on their first mortgage years ago. Refinancing the whole loan to pull out equity means giving up that rate on the entire balance. A second mortgage isolates the new, higher rate to just the new funds being borrowed.

Common Uses on Investment Properties

  • Down payment for another property — using equity in one investment to acquire the next
  • Renovation or value-add capital — funding improvements that increase rent or resale value
  • Bridge financing — short-term funds while a sale or refinance on another property closes
  • Debt consolidation — rolling higher-interest debt into a lower-cost, equity-secured loan

What Lenders Look At

Because investment properties don't get the same insured, low-down-payment treatment as an owner-occupied home, second mortgage lenders on rental properties focus heavily on:

  1. Combined loan-to-value (CLTV) — the first and second mortgage together, against current appraised value
  2. Rental income — actual lease income, often at a discounted rate to account for vacancy and expenses
  3. Property condition and location — directly affects appraised value and marketability
  4. Your overall portfolio — if you hold multiple investment properties, lenders will look at your total exposure
Common mistake: Treating a second mortgage as free money because it's "just equity." It's still debt secured against the property, with real payments and real risk if the investment doesn't perform as expected. Run the numbers on the new payment against the property's actual cash flow before committing.

Private vs. Institutional Second Mortgages

Some second mortgages on investment properties come from institutional lenders, others from private lenders or MICs — particularly when the deal needs to close fast or the borrower's income documentation doesn't fit standard underwriting. The right source depends on your timeline, your equity position, and how the rest of your financial picture looks. This is a deal worth having a mortgage agent map out rather than shopping rate-first.

How This Fits an Investment Strategy

For active investors, a second mortgage is often one tool among several — used alongside refinancing, HELOCs, and new acquisitions to keep capital moving across a portfolio. The agents who add the most value here aren't just finding one loan; they're helping sequence financing across multiple properties so each move sets up the next one.
