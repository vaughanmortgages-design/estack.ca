How to Buy Gold Bullion in Canada (2026 Guide)

Updated June 2026 · 7 min read · estack.ca editorial team

In 2026 — with inflation memories still fresh and equity markets volatile — more Canadians than ever are adding physical gold to their portfolios. Not ETFs. Not mining stocks. Real, tangible bullion you can hold. This guide covers everything you need to know: why Canadians buy it, coins vs bars, where to find it, and how to store and tax it properly.

Why Canadians Buy Physical Gold

Physical gold appeals to a specific type of Canadian investor — typically self-employed professionals, tradespeople, and small business owners who have seen what happens when paper wealth evaporates quickly.

The main reasons Canadians choose bullion over other gold exposure:

  • Inflation hedge. Gold has historically maintained purchasing power when the Canadian dollar loses ground. After the inflation spikes of 2022–2024, many investors added physical gold as insurance.
  • No counterparty risk. A gold bar in your possession has no issuer risk, no brokerage failure risk, no platform dependency. It is simply the asset itself.
  • Portfolio diversification. Gold often moves inversely to equities, smoothing out volatility in a balanced portfolio.
  • Privacy and tangibility. For self-employed Canadians who distrust institutional systems, physical gold offers a form of wealth that doesn't live on a server.
  • Generational transfer. Physical gold passes easily between generations without intermediaries or complex estate mechanics.

Gold is not a get-rich-quick play. It is a long-term preservation tool. Treated as such, it has a legitimate role in a Canadian investor's portfolio.

Gold Coins vs Gold Bars — Which Is Better?

The coins-vs-bars question is the first decision every new bullion buyer faces. Both are solid choices; the right answer depends on your goals.

Format Pros Cons Best For
Gold Coins Easier to sell in smaller increments; legal tender status; widely recognized; strong resale liquidity Higher premium over spot price; cost more per ounce than bars Flexibility, smaller purchases, gifting, resale ease
Gold Bars Lower premium per ounce; efficient for larger holdings; straightforward valuation Harder to sell in partial amounts; less recognizable to casual buyers Larger capital allocation, long-term storage, cost efficiency

General rule: If you're buying less than 5 oz, coins offer better flexibility. For larger purchases — 10 oz or more — bars typically give you more gold per dollar. Many serious investors hold both.

Popular Canadian coins include the Maple Leaf (Royal Canadian Mint), which is one of the most recognized and liquid gold coins in the world. Popular bar brands include PAMP Suisse, Royal Canadian Mint, and Valcambi.

Royal Canadian Mint Coins Explained

The Royal Canadian Mint (RCM) is one of the most respected government mints in the world, and its flagship product — the Canadian Gold Maple Leaf — is among the most recognized and liquid bullion coins globally.

Canadian Gold Maple Leaf

The Gold Maple Leaf has been produced since 1979 and is minted in .9999 fine gold — one of the highest purities available in a government-issued coin. Available in five sizes (1 oz, 1/2 oz, 1/4 oz, 1/10 oz, 1/20 oz), the 1 oz version is the most popular for investors. Since 2018, Maple Leafs include MintShield technology, which reduces milk spots and preserves surface quality.

Other RCM Gold Products

  • Gold Bars: 1 oz and 100 oz bars, sold with a certificate of authenticity.
  • Collector Coins: Limited mintage coins with premium above bullion value — less liquid than standard Maple Leafs but collectible.
  • Gold Wafers: 1g, 2.5g, and 5g wafers for small-increment buyers.

You can buy RCM products directly from the Mint's website, through authorized dealers, or on eBay Canada where secondary market pricing often undercuts the Mint's retail prices.

Storage and Insurance Basics

Buying physical gold is the easy part. Storing it safely is where many buyers underinvest — and where losses actually happen.

At-Home Storage

A quality home safe is sufficient for modest holdings — typically under $10,000–$15,000 in value. Look for a fire-rated, bolted-to-floor safe from a recognized brand (Gardall, Liberty, Fort Knox). Your home insurance may or may not cover bullion; most standard policies have low sublimits ($1,000–$2,000) for precious metals.

Before storing at home: Call your insurance broker and ask specifically about scheduled bullion coverage. Premiums are typically low for significant added protection.

Bank Safe Deposit Box

A safe deposit box at a Canadian bank is a common middle ground — physically secure, not insured by the bank, but far harder to access than your home. Note that safe deposit box contents are not covered by CDIC or bank insurance.

Professional Vault Storage

For holdings above $25,000, professional vault storage through Kitco, IDS, or Brinks is worth considering. These services offer segregated or allocated storage, full replacement-value insurance, and simplified resale directly from the vault. Fees typically run 0.5–1.0% annually — cheap peace of mind for serious investors.

Tax Treatment in Canada (Capital Gains)

Physical gold is treated as a capital property under the Income Tax Act. This has meaningful implications for Canadian investors.

Capital Gains on Sale

When you sell gold bullion at a profit, you realize a capital gain. In Canada, capital gains are only partially included in income — the inclusion rate determines how much of your gain is taxable.

As of 2026, the inclusion rate is 2/3 (66.67%) for gains above $250,000 per year (for individuals), and 1/2 (50%) on the first $250,000. This reflects the federal budget changes announced in 2024 and effective for dispositions after June 2024.

No GST/HST on Investment Gold

Investment-grade gold (99.5%+ purity) is zero-rated for GST/HST in Canada — you do not pay sales tax on the purchase. This is a significant advantage over other collectibles.

Record-Keeping

Keep documentation of every gold purchase: date, price paid, and quantity. The adjusted cost base (ACB) determines your capital gain at sale. Without records, CRA may assess the full proceeds as gain — a costly mistake.

Note: This is general information, not tax advice. Speak with a Canadian tax professional before making investment decisions with significant tax implications.
