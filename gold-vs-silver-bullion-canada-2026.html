Gold vs. silver bullion in Canada: what to compare before you buy

Spot price matters, but premiums, storage, resale demand and dealer reputation can matter just as much.

Gold and silver can look similar on a price chart, but they behave very differently in a buyer's hands. Gold is compact and easier to store in higher dollar amounts. Silver usually has a lower entry price, but it takes up far more space and can carry larger percentage premiums.

1. Compare the premium, not only the spot price

The premium is the amount charged above the metal's market price. It covers fabrication, distribution, dealer costs and market demand. A product with a lower sticker price can still be more expensive on a percentage basis if the premium is high.

Compare the dealer's total checkout price against the live spot price, then calculate the percentage premium. Also check whether shipping, insurance or payment-method fees are added later.

2. Coins versus bars

Government-minted coins such as the Canadian Maple Leaf are widely recognized and may be easier to resell. Bars can offer lower premiums, especially at larger sizes, but brand recognition and assay packaging matter.

New buyers often start with familiar sizes rather than a single very large bar because smaller units can be easier to sell in portions.

3. Storage and insurance

Gold is easier to store discreetly because a meaningful dollar value occupies little space. Silver becomes heavy and bulky quickly. Before buying either metal, decide whether you will use a home safe, a bank safety-deposit box or professional vault storage.

Check insurance limits. A standard home policy may provide limited coverage for precious metals, cash and collectibles.

4. Liquidity and the dealer's buyback policy

Before buying, ask how the dealer handles resale. Compare the expected buyback spread, required identification, payment timing and whether the dealer buys products purchased elsewhere.

A low purchase premium is useful, but the round-trip cost matters more: what you pay to buy plus what you give up when selling.

5. Canadian tax considerations

Certain qualifying investment-grade precious metals may receive favourable sales-tax treatment in Canada, while collectible or lower-purity products may be treated differently. Tax treatment can depend on purity and product type, so verify the current rules before purchasing.

6. Check the dealer, not just the product

Look for transparent pricing, secure checkout, clear shipping policies, insured delivery, reachable customer support and a visible buyback process. Avoid pressure tactics and claims that prices can only go up.
