Construction Financing for GTA Developers: How Draw Mortgages Work

Updated June 2026  ·  8 min read  ·  estack.ca editorial team

More small builders and infill developers stall on financing logistics than on actual construction problems. Construction financing isn't a lump sum — it's a structured release of funds tied to your build progress, and understanding how draws actually work is the difference between a project that flows and one that's constantly waiting on money.

How a Construction (Draw) Mortgage Works

Instead of receiving the full loan amount at closing like a standard mortgage, construction financing releases funds in stages — "draws" — as the build hits agreed milestones. You pay interest only on the funds actually drawn, not the full approved amount, which keeps carrying costs lower in the early stages of a project.

Typical Draw StageWhat's Inspected
Foundation / footingsExcavation complete, foundation poured
FramingStructure framed, roof on, building enclosed
Lock-upWindows, doors, exterior cladding complete
Rough-inPlumbing, electrical, HVAC roughed in
FinalOccupancy-ready, final inspection passed
From the trades side: Every draw typically requires a third-party progress inspection before funds release — budget the time and minor cost for this into your schedule. Builders who plan for inspection timing rather than treating it as a surprise keep their draws moving on schedule.

What Lenders Require Before They'll Fund

  • Detailed cost breakdown — a line-item budget covering every phase of the build, not a rough estimate
  • Approved building permits and any required municipal approvals for the GTA municipality you're building in
  • Fixed-price contracts with licensed trades and contractors, or a documented self-build plan if you're acting as your own GC
  • Builder's risk insurance covering the property during construction
  • A realistic timeline — lenders want to see the project completing within the financing term, with contingency built in

Who Qualifies

Construction financing in the GTA generally falls into a few buyer profiles:

  1. Owner-builders constructing a custom home for themselves
  2. Small infill developers building 1–4 units for sale
  3. Tradespeople and contractors building their own spec home or duplex, sometimes self-performing parts of the work
  4. Larger developers on multi-unit projects, who typically move into commercial construction financing structures
Common mistake: Underestimating soft costs — permits, engineering, surveys, development charges, and contingency. Lenders will scrutinize a budget that only accounts for materials and labour and leaves no room for the rest.

Construction-to-Permanent vs. Construction-Only

Some lenders offer a single product that converts to a standard mortgage automatically once the build is complete — useful for owner-builders who want one closing instead of two. Others fund construction only, requiring you to arrange separate "takeout" financing once the project is done. Knowing which structure you're getting matters for planning your exit and avoiding a financing gap at completion.

Self-Employed and Trades-Specific Considerations

If you're a tradesperson building your own project — acting as GC, self-performing some of the work, or running the build through your own contracting business — lenders will want extra clarity on how your time and labour are being valued in the budget, and how that interacts with your other income. This is exactly the kind of file that benefits from a mortgage agent who actually understands how a trades business runs, rather than treating self-performed labour as a red flag.
