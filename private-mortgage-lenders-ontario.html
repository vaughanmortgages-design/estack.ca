Private Mortgage Lenders in Ontario: When Banks Say No

Updated June 2026  ·  8 min read  ·  estack.ca editorial team

When an A lender or B lender turns you down, the conversation isn't over — it just moves to a different room. Private mortgage lending is a legitimate, regulated corner of Ontario's mortgage market, used every year by self-employed borrowers, people rebuilding credit, and investors who need to move fast on a deal a bank can't underwrite in time.

It's also widely misunderstood. Here's what private lending actually is, what it costs, and when it makes sense.

What "Private Lender" Actually Means

In Ontario, private mortgages are funded by individuals, mortgage investment corporations (MICs), or syndicated groups of investors — not banks or credit unions. They lend primarily against the equity in the property, not your income or credit history. That's the entire appeal: a private lender cares far less about your T4 and far more about the loan-to-value (LTV) ratio.

Private lenders are licensed and regulated through FSRA (the Financial Services Regulatory Authority of Ontario), and any mortgage agent arranging the deal — like a broker working with you — must be licensed too. This isn't the unregulated grey market some people imagine.

Who Actually Uses Private Mortgages

  • Self-employed borrowers whose write-offs make their stated income look smaller than their real cash flow
  • Borrowers rebuilding credit after a consumer proposal, bankruptcy, or rough financial stretch
  • Investors who need to close in days, not weeks, to win a deal
  • Homeowners needing a short-term bridge between selling one property and closing on another
  • People with non-traditional income — new immigrants, gig workers, or those with irregular contract income
What it's not for: A permanent solution. Private mortgages are priced and structured as a bridge — usually 6 months to 2 years — to get you to a point where an A or B lender will take you on.

What It Actually Costs

Private mortgage rates in Ontario typically run higher than bank rates, reflecting the higher risk and faster, more flexible underwriting. Expect lender fees and broker fees on top of the rate — these are usually rolled into the mortgage rather than paid out of pocket. The exact numbers move with the rate environment, so get current quotes rather than anchoring to anything you read online, including here.

The One Question That Matters Most: Your Exit

Every legitimate private mortgage conversation starts with the same question: what's your plan to get out of this loan? A good mortgage agent won't put you into a private deal without a clear path — refinancing once your credit recovers, selling the property, or paying it off when a contract pays out. Private lending without an exit strategy is how people get stuck.

Trades & self-employed tip: If you're using a private mortgage as a bridge while your business builds a two-year income history, that history clock is running the moment you start. Many borrowers move from private to B lender to A lender over a 2–3 year stretch as their paper trail catches up to their real income.

How to Vet a Private Lender

  1. Work through a licensed mortgage agent or broker — not a direct private lender you found through a classified ad.
  2. Confirm FSRA licensing on the brokerage and the agent arranging the deal.
  3. Get all fees in writing before signing — lender fee, broker fee, legal fee, any renewal fee.
  4. Read the prepayment terms — some private mortgages have an open structure, others penalize early payout.
  5. Have your own real estate lawyer review the commitment before you sign.

Private vs. Second Mortgage

A private mortgage can sit in first or second position. If you already have a bank mortgage and need additional funds without disturbing it, a private second mortgage behind your existing first is common — and often faster than refinancing the whole thing. Bottom Line

Private mortgage lending in Ontario isn't a last resort for people who can't get approved anywhere — it's a tool, used strategically, by borrowers whose financial story doesn't fit a bank's checkbox underwriting.
