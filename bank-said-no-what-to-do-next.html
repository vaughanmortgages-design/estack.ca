Bank Said No? Here's What to Do Next

estack.caJune 20267 min read

Getting declined by a bank is not a dead end. In Canada, most people who get turned down by the big banks get approved elsewhere — often at rates that aren't far off. The problem is they don't know where to look, or they give up after the first no.

Why Banks Say No to Good Borrowers

Canada's major banks use rigid qualification criteria that penalize perfectly capable borrowers. Self-employed income, T4 gaps, recent career changes, credit issues from years ago, or simply a complicated income structure can all trigger a decline — even if you have the income and assets to service the loan comfortably.

Step 1 — Understand Why You Were Declined

Before doing anything else, get the reason in writing. Canadian lenders are required to tell you why you were declined. The reason determines your strategy: a credit score issue requires a different fix than an income documentation issue.

Step 2 — Pull Your Credit Report

Get your full credit report from both Equifax and TransUnion — free at annualcreditreport.com. Look for errors, old collections, or accounts you don't recognize. Disputing legitimate errors can improve your score within 30–60 days.

Step 3 — Talk to a Mortgage Broker, Not Another Bank

A mortgage broker has access to dozens of lenders — not just one. That includes B lenders, credit unions, trust companies, and private lenders who all have different qualification criteria. One broker conversation replaces five bank applications and multiple hard credit pulls.

Step 4 — If You Need Time, Build Your File

If you're 6–18 months away from being ready, use that time strategically:

  • Pay down revolving credit below 30% utilization
  • Don't apply for any new credit — each hard pull lowers your score
  • If self-employed, ensure your last two NOAs show consistent or growing income
  • Save the largest down payment you can manage
